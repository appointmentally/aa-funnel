If you judge your Facebook and Instagram ads by cost-per-lead, you're almost certainly funding the wrong ones. It feels great to see leads coming in at $15 or $20 a piece — but a cheap lead that never shows up to your showroom isn't a bargain. It's the most expensive kind of empty calendar there is.

Here's the uncomfortable truth we see again and again: the cheapest leads usually cost you the most.

The number you're watching is the wrong one

Cost-per-lead measures how cheaply you can get someone to type their name into a form. It says nothing about whether that person owns a flat, has keys in hand, or will actually walk into your showroom and sign. Across the 69 firms we've worked with, the ad with the lowest cost-per-lead and the ad that produces the lowest cost-per-booked-consultation are routinely different ads — and sometimes they're the exact opposite.

That means the firm reading its dashboard "lowest cost-per-lead = best ad" is frequently pouring budget into the ad that fills the calendar with no-shows, and quietly switching off the "expensive" ad that was actually booking real appointments.

Why your cheapest leads ghost

Cheap leads are cheap for a reason. A hook that promises a free gift, a giveaway, or a vague "learn more" pulls in a flood of low-intent clicks — people who were curious, not committed. They cost little because half of them were never going to renovate this year. The form fills up, your cost-per-lead drops, and your designers' calendars stay empty.

The opposite is also true: an ad that asks more of the viewer — that filters for someone with a real budget and a real timeline — will often show a higher cost-per-lead and a far better cost-per-booked. You paid more per name, but those names show up.

The two numbers that actually matter

Stop ranking your ads by lead price. Rank them by two things instead:

1. Cost-per-booked-consultation. Take what you spent on an ad and divide it by the number of booked, showed-up appointments it produced — not the leads, the appointments. This is the number that maps to revenue.

2. Lead-to-appointment rate. Of the leads an ad generated, what percentage actually became a showroom appointment? A "$20 lead" ad with a 10% booking rate is far worse than a "$45 lead" ad booking at 40% — and the dashboard will never tell you that unless you look.

How to find your real cost-per-booked

You don't need fancy software. You need to connect two things your business already records: ad spend per campaign, and showroom appointments that actually happened. Once a week, for each ad or campaign, divide the spend by the number of consults that showed up. The ranking you get will look nothing like your cost-per-lead ranking — and it will tell you exactly where your money should go.

One caution: this only works if your appointment outcomes are honest. If no one updates whether each booking actually showed, your numbers are fiction (more on that in a future post). Reconcile "did they show?" every week, or you're optimising against noise.

What to do on Monday

Pull your last 30 days. For every ad still running, work out its cost-per-booked-appointment and its lead-to-appointment rate. You'll almost certainly find one "expensive" ad that's secretly your best booker, and one "cheap" ad that's burning budget on names that never arrive. Shift spend toward the bookers — even if their leads look pricier — and watch your showroom, not your cost-per-lead, fill up.

Frequently asked questions

What's a good cost-per-booked appointment for an interior design firm?
It depends on your average project value and close rate — but the right benchmark is always your own: track it for a month, then judge every new ad against that baseline rather than a number you read online.

Should I just kill every high-cost-per-lead ad?
No — that's exactly the trap. Some of your highest-cost-per-lead ads are your best bookers. Kill ads with a high cost-per-booked, not a high cost-per-lead.

Isn't a cheaper lead always at least a little better?
Only if it shows up. A lead that never becomes an appointment has a cost-per-booked of infinity, no matter how cheap the click was.

My leads are cheap but no one books — is it the ad?
Maybe not. Cheap leads that don't convert are often a follow-up or booking problem, not a creative one. Fix where the funnel actually leaks before you brief a new ad.
