Why renovation portal leads stop working

Most firms start by buying leads from renovation portals. The problem is structural: the same lead is sold to four, five, sometimes seven firms at once. By the time you call, the homeowner has already been messaged by everyone else, and you're competing on price before you've said a word.

You end up paying for contact details, not customers — and most of those contacts never show up to anything.

The shift: stop buying leads, start buying showed-up appointments

A better model is pay-per-show: instead of paying for leads, you only pay when a homeowner actually shows up to your showroom. No retainer. No paying for no-shows. You're buying outcomes, not phone numbers.

This flips the incentive. The people generating your appointments only get paid when those appointments turn up — so everything is engineered around show rate, not lead volume.

How showed-up appointments are actually generated

Filling a showroom predictably comes down to a few moving parts working together:

  1. A sharp offer and message aimed at homeowners who are ready to renovate now — not "someday" browsers.
  2. Paid social campaigns (Facebook & Instagram) that put that offer in front of the right Singapore homeowners and are managed daily, not set-and-forget.
  3. Fast, structured follow-up. A booked lead means nothing if no one reaches them quickly — homeowners are hottest within the first few minutes of enquiring.
  4. Qualified, exclusive appointments booked straight into your calendar — never shared with another firm.
  5. Reminders and confirmation so the appointment actually shows up, not just gets booked.

When these run together, the number that matters — appointments that physically show up — goes up.

Why "booked" doesn't mean "showed up"

The biggest hidden leak for most firms is the gap between a booking and a show. An appointment that's booked but never confirmed, reminded, or nurtured quietly disappears. Fixing the show rate is often worth more than generating more leads in the first place — it's the difference between a busy calendar and a busy showroom.
